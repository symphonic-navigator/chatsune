# End-to-End Encryption of Your Chatsune Data — Phase 1

Here is what Claude and I are implementing today.

A number of you have asked for a stronger privacy guarantee than "trust the operator not to peek". So we are building one. The design is finalised, and the plumbing lands in the next release.

The goal, once the rollout is complete: everything you own inside Chatsune — chat messages, memory entries, journal content, uploaded files, avatars, artefacts — will be sealed with a key that only your password can unlock. A stolen database dump, a leaked environment file, a compromised backup tape, or even physical theft of the server in cold state will all yield unreadable bytes. I, as the operator, will not be able to read your data either, and this is verifiable by code review: the server never stores a copy of the key material needed to decrypt your data, and the master key in the environment file is used only for operational data such as provider credentials — not for anything that belongs to you. The one honest caveat is that while your session is live, the server has to hold your key in memory so it can actually do things on your behalf (LLM calls, memory consolidation, embeddings). Live-server memory inspection during an active session is out of scope for this model; what we are protecting is everything at rest.

Today's work is Phase 1 — the foundation. We are building the key-management plumbing: the collection that holds each user's sealed key, the login flow that unseals it, the signup flow that generates it. No existing data is encrypted by this release. The switch-over to actually sealing each kind of data — messages first, then memory, then files and everything else — will happen collection by collection in later releases, with the machinery already in place and tested against real traffic.

A word on the **recovery key**, because it is the most important thing for you to understand. The key that protects your data is derived from your password. That is what makes the guarantee work: nobody else can derive it. The unavoidable consequence is that if you forget your password, there is no-one — not me, not the database, not Claude — who can give you your data back. That is the whole point. To give you a safety net that does not compromise that guarantee, every account gets a **32-character recovery key** at the moment it is created. It is generated in your browser, shown to you exactly once, and then gone from our side. Put it in your password manager. It behaves like a second password that only you hold: your data-key is sealed twice on the server, once under a derivative of your password and once under a derivative of your recovery key, so either one unlocks it — but we never retain the recovery key in a form we can use later. The plaintext passes through a single wrap operation at signup and is then forgotten server-side. After that, the only copy exists on your side.

If you lose your password, you use your recovery key to get back in and to set a new password. If you lose both, your data is unrecoverable. I would rather be upfront about that than pretend otherwise. Your account shell stays (username, e-mail, login ability after an admin reset), but anything that was encrypted is gone. A future admin-side "destroy and reactivate" flow will let you come back with a clean, empty account without leaking anything about the old one.

**For existing accounts:** there is a one-time migration on your next login after the update. The browser will generate your recovery key on the fly, and you will see the same "save this" modal that new users see. Nothing to do ahead of time; just make sure you are ready to save it properly when it appears.

Day to day, almost nothing changes. Login takes about 300 ms longer under the hood (key derivation runs in a browser worker so the UI stays smooth), and right after signup or your first post-update login you will meet the recovery-key modal once. After that, Chatsune behaves as before — until a later release quietly starts storing your first encrypted collection, and nothing about how you use the app needs to change for that either.
